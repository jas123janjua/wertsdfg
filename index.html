ABOUT WHALES

A diversified and widely dispersed group of totally aquatic placental marine mammals are whales. They relate to huge members of the infraorder Cetacea, which includes all cetaceans other than dolphins and porpoises, as an informal and colloquial grouping. Formally speaking, porpoises and dolphins can be categorized as whales. Even-toed ungulates make to the order Cetartiodactyla, which includes whales, dolphins, and porpoises. Their closest extant non-cetacean cousins are the hippopotamuses, from which they separated around 54 million years ago along with other cetaceans. The last common ancestor of the two whale parvorders, baleen whales (Mysticeti) and toothed whales (Odontoceti), is estimated to have lived around 34 million years ago. Four current (living) families are comprised in the Mysticetes: Balaenopteridae (the rorquals), Balaenidae (right whales), whale), as well as the grey whale, Eschrichtiidae. Odontocetes comprise the six families of dolphins and porpoises that are not technically whales, as well as the Monodontidae (belugas and narwhals), Physeteridae (the sperm whale), Kogiidae (the dwarf and pygmy sperm whale), and Ziphiidae (the beaked whales).

ORCA

The biggest dolphin in the marine dolphin family is the toothed orca (Orcinus orca), often known as the killer whale. In the genus Orcinus, it is the sole surviving species. Orcas are distinguished by their bodies with patterns of black and white. They are a widely distributed species that may be found in a variety of marine habitats, including tropical oceans, the Arctic, and the Antarctic. Apex predators, orcas have a varied diet. Individual populations frequently focus on certain kinds of prey. This comprises a wide range of fish, sharks, rays, and marine mammals, including whales, dolphins, and seals. They have strong social ties; certain populations are made up of matrilineal family units that are remarkably persistent (pods). Their advanced hunting strategies and vocalizations, which are frequently inherited from their kin and are passed down through generations, are seen as examples of animal civilization.

SPERM WHALE

The biggest member of the toothed whale family and the greatest predator is the sperm whale, or cachalot[a] (Physeter macrocephalus). Along with the pygmy sperm whale and dwarf sperm whale of the genus Kogia, it is one of the three surviving species in the sperm whale family and the only living member of the genus Physeter.A pelagic animal with a global range, sperm whales travel periodically in order to forage and reproduce. Mature males, or bulls, live alone outside of the mating season, whereas females and young males reside in groups. The females work together to safeguard and care for their young. Every four to twenty years, females give birth, and they raise the calves for almost ten years. Mature sperm whales have no natural predators, while weaker adults and calves can occasionally be killed by killer whale (orca) pods.

BELUGA WHALE

The beluga whale (/bɪˈluːɡə/;[4] Delphinapterus leucas) is an Arctic and sub-Arctic cetacean. It is one of two members of the family Monodontidae, along with the narwhal, and the only member of the genus Delphinapterus. It is also known as the white whale, as it is the only cetacean to regularly occur with this colour; the sea canary, due to its high-pitched calls; and the melonhead, though that more commonly refers to the melon-headed whale, which is an oceanic dolphin.

Humpback whale

Among the baleen whale species is the humpback whale (Megaptera novaeangliae). It is the sole species in the genus Megaptera and a rorqual, belonging to the Balaenopteridae family. Adults may weigh up to 40 metric tons (44 short tons) and vary in length from 14 to 17 meters (46 to 56 feet). The humpback's body is characterized by its large pectoral fins and tubercles on top of its skull. It is well-liked by whale watchers because to its reputation for breaching and other unusual surface movements. A difficult song by a man usually lasts between four and thirty-three minutes.

BLUE WHALE

The baleen whale, or blue whale (Balaenoptera musculus), is a kind of marine animal. It is the biggest known animal to have ever lived, with a maximum proven length of 29.9 meters (98 feet) and a maximum weight of 199 tonnes (196 long tons; 219 short tons).[3][A] The long, thin body of a blue whale can have several dorsal tints of greyish-blue and differ somewhat in color beneath. There are four subspecies known to science: B. m. brevicauda (also known as the pygmy blue whale) in the Indian and South Pacific oceans, B. m. musculus in the North Atlantic and North Pacific, B. m. intermedia in the Southern Ocean, and B. m. indica in the Northern Indian Ocean. A population that could represent a fifth subspecies is also present in the seas off the coast of Chile.

BOWHEAD WHALE

The only surviving member of the genus Balaena is the bowhead whale (Balaena mysticetus), a species of baleen whale that belongs to the family Balaenidae. It gets its name from its large triangular skull, which it utilizes to break through Arctic ice. It is the only baleen whale that is unique to the Arctic and subarctic seas. The species was also known by the names steeple-top, polar, Greenland right whale, and Arctic whale.

GREY WHALE

A baleen whale that migrates yearly between feeding and breeding areas is the grey whale (Eschrichtius robustus), often referred to as the grey whale,grey back whale, Pacific grey whale, Korean grey whale, or California grey whale. It can grow up to 14.9 meters (49 feet) in length, weigh up to 41 tonnes (90,000 pounds), and have a lifespan of 55 to 70 years, with the exception of one female who was believed to be between 75 and 80 years old. The whale's dark skin has white mottling and grey patches, which give rise to its popular name.
